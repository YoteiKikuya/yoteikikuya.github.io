SLEEPLESS CITY

Project Type:
Tabletop Game
Organization:
Ninth Layer (Independent Studio), Simon Fraser University
Roles:
Art Direction, Character Art, Graphic Design, Balancing
Software:
Krita, Figma, Adobe InDesign
Duration:
February 2026 - April 2026
Collaborators:
Ben Nichols, Caleb Schelp, Kathryn Sikich, Geoffrey Nathan

In Spring 2026, I was tasked with designing a tabletop game in a team for IAT 312 Foundations of Game Design. As lead character artist, my role was to create player and enemy cards that enriched the core gameplay loop and added an emergent narrative hook.

Sleepless City is a two-player cooperate card battler, where players would build up rosters of expressive characters and help each other fight through hordes of robots. To scope out the project, I made myself a checklist:

26 Survivors to Embody

It was important to give players a wide selection across all demographics, so everyone has someone to root for. All characters should seem capable of working together to some extent.

Click here to see the full roster

6 Enemy Types to Adapt To

This roster needed to be diverse enough to remain engaging, but share enough in common to separate them from the survivors. Ultimately, they are components of a larger machine.

3 Bosses to Conquer

Players choose the boss, and build the world deck above it. Each comes with its own unique aesthetic, story potential and challenges.

1 Month to Complete

In order to give my team enough time for reworking and balance, I aimed to finish all my assets within this period. On development days, this meant designing 1-3 characters per session.


On early drafts, the plan was for every character to share a colour palette, portrait angle and graphical elements for a more traditional playing card feel. However, this approach flattened the characters to instruments. I pivoted to each character having a unique aesthetic makeup that reflects and gives clues about their backgrounds. This also helped to communicate the "greater than the sum of our parts" messaging by having these disparate cards working together.

Throughout development, the artwork remained a consistently well-received element and a defining part of the game's identity. I observed players building their rosters around self-directed themes, such as one player making an aquatic-themed team, and another forming a machine cult. Overall, the players used the artwork as a springboard for their own imaginations, which aligned with my design intent.
